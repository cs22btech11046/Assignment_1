\textbf{10.15.1.25: Question}. Which of the following arguments are correct and which are not correct?Give reasons for your answer.
(i) If two coins are tossed simultaneously there are three possible outcome s —two heads, two tails or one of each.Therefore, for each of these outcomes, the probability is $\frac{1}{3}$.

(ii) If a die is thrown, there are two possible outcomes— an odd number or an even number. Therefore, the probability of getting an odd number is $\frac{1}{2}$.

\textbf{Solution}:

(i) When two coins are tossed simultaneously, the possible outcomes are (H, H), (H, T), (T, H), (T, T), which are all equally likely. 
     Therefore,

\begin{align*}
    \pr{\text{getting two heads}} &= \frac{1}{4}\\
    \pr{\text{getting two tails}} &= \frac{1}{4}\\
    \pr{\text{getting one of each}} &= \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}
\end{align*}
\textbf{Explanation:} Event with one of each head and tail contains two mutually exclusive events (H, T), (T, H) .Therefore, probality of getting one of each is $\frac{1}{2}$ and not $\frac{1}{3}$.
\\So, the above statement is incorrect.

\vspace{\baselineskip}
(ii) Here, let E be the event ‘getting an odd number’. The number of possible outcomes when a die is thrown is six : 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 and 6, and the outcomes favourable to E are 1, 3 and 6. Therefore, the number of outcomes favourable to E is 3. So,
\pr{E} = $\frac{\text{Number of outcomes favourable to } E}{\text{Number of all possible outcomes}}$
\begin{align*}
 \pr{\text{E}} &= \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}
\end{align*}
Therefore, the probability of getting an odd number is $\frac{1}{2}$.

\textbf{Explanation:} Event of getting an odd number and Event of getting an even number are equally likely and they together forms an exhaustive event.
Hence,
\begin{align*}
	\pr{\text{getting an odd number}} &= \frac{1}{2}\\
	\pr{\text{getting an even number}} &= \frac{1}{2}
\end{align*}
\\So the above statement is correct.
